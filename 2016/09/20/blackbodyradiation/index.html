Back in the day (1900) some sound fella’ called Planck came along and solved the ‘ultraviolet catastrophe’, which was this big deal with not being able to achieve mathematically the same energy density that was being shown experimentally. Basically at low enough frequencies all went well and the ‘classical’ spectrum approaches the experimental results, but as the frequency increases, shit goes cray and out pops infinity. And thus came the beginning of the end of all that favours reason.
So Planck being a cool guy, came along and said “fuck it” and decided ,against his better judgement, to assume that the energy could only take on discrete (and uniform) energy values. So after tedious experimentation he found that energy packets were proportional to frequency ($\Delta E \propto v$) and that those energy packets were equal to h times frequency ($\Delta E = hv$)

Planck’s Law

The following equation is the beginning of our modern physics, it describes the amount of energy a black body gives off at a given temperature through different wavelengths.

$$
B_\lambda(\lambda,T) =\frac{2 hc^2}{\lambda^5}\frac{1}{ e^{\frac{hc}{\lambda kT}}-1}
$$

Setting up the code

To start things off it makes sense to put all of the above equation into its function as it will be called plenty of times through a variety of increasing wavelengths. The minimum range for the wavelength is set just above zero to avoid any dividing by 0 errors.

Now to design a loop which plots as many unique temperature density plots as you want. In this case I choose the for the range to be 5, to avoid excess garbage. plt.hold() determines whether or not the previous line gets deleted ot not.
